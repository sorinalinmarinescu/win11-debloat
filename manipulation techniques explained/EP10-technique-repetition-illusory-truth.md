# Episode 10 — Technique #9: Repetition & the Illusory-Truth Effect
### Why a thing repeated enough starts to feel true

**Block:** 1 · **Technique 9 of 20** · **Target runtime:** 12–14 min
**Anchor callback:** Campaign A — the endlessly repeated slogan "It's toasted."
**Logline:** Repetition does two jobs: it makes you *like* things (the more familiar, the more comfortable) and it makes you *believe* things (the more often you hear a claim, the truer it feels) — even when you know better.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define the *mere-exposure effect* and the *illusory-truth effect*.
- Understand why familiarity feels like both safety and truth.
- Build a defense against the slogan, the talking point, and the repeated lie.

## COLD OPEN

[GFX: kinetic typography — "IT'S TOASTED" repeats, multiplies, fills the screen.]

**VO:** "It's toasted." For decades, a cigarette brand repeated those two words. Toasted — so what? Most tobacco is heat-treated. The phrase told you almost nothing. But it was everywhere, so often, for so long, that it lodged in millions of minds and quietly said: *this one's smoother, this one's fine.*

[Beat.]

**VO:** Repetition is the cheapest trick in the book, and one of the most powerful — because of two quirks in your brain that have been measured in the lab.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Mere-exposure effect:** we tend to *like* things more simply because they're familiar.
**Illusory-truth effect:** we tend to *believe* claims more simply because we've heard them repeated.

**VO:** Two separate effects, one engine: familiarity. The psychologist Robert Zajonc showed in 1968 that just being exposed to something repeatedly — a shape, a word, a face — makes people prefer it, with no other reason. That's the mere-exposure effect, and it's why a jingle you "hate" can still make you reach for the product.

[LOWER THIRD: Mere-exposure effect — Robert Zajonc, 1968]

**VO:** The more dangerous cousin was shown in 1977 by researchers Hasher, Goldstein, and Toppino: people rated repeated statements as more *true* than new ones — regardless of whether they actually were true. Repeat a claim, and it gains "the ring of truth." That's the illusory-truth effect.

[LOWER THIRD: Illusory-truth effect — Hasher, Goldstein & Toppino, 1977]

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a brain "processing" a new sentence with effort; then the same sentence again, gliding through smoothly; a meter labeled "feels true" rises.]

**VO:** Here's the unsettling mechanism. When your brain processes something easily — smoothly, without friction — it reads that ease as a signal of truth and safety. Scientists call this "fluency." A claim you've heard ten times is *easy* to process the eleventh time. Your brain mistakes that easiness for evidence: *I recognize this, so it's probably right.*

**VO:** The catch is that recognition has nothing to do with accuracy. A lie you've heard ten times also becomes easy to process — and so it, too, starts to feel true. Studies find the effect can work even on people who, when asked directly, know the correct answer. The familiar version *feels* right even as you know it's wrong. Repetition doesn't bother to defeat your knowledge; it sneaks underneath it.

**VO:** This is why "repeat a lie often enough and it becomes accepted" isn't just a cynical saying — it's a measurable feature of human minds. And it's why propagandists and advertisers alike have always loved the simple, repeated phrase.

---

## SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign

[B-ROLL: vintage "It's toasted" ads across magazines, posters, radio.]

**VO:** "It's toasted" is mere exposure and illusory truth working together. The phrase was nearly content-free, but it was repeated across every medium for years. Familiarity bred liking — the brand felt like an old friend. And the vague health-flavored implication, repeated endlessly, came to *feel* true: that this cigarette was somehow smoother, gentler, safer. None of it required evidence. It only required repetition and time.

---

## SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today

[B-ROLL: montage — jingles; slogans; a politician repeating the exact same phrase in five interviews; the same false claim across many websites.]

**VO:** **Advertising.** The jingle, the slogan, the brand name said three times in a fifteen-second spot — all mere exposure. The aim isn't to convince you today; it's to make the brand so familiar that, in the half-second you choose at the shelf, your hand drifts to the one you "know."

**VO:** **Politics.** Watch how disciplined campaigns repeat an identical phrase — word for word — across every spokesperson and every interview. That's not a lack of imagination; it's engineering. They're banking the illusory-truth effect, getting the phrase to feel true through sheer repetition. The same mechanism powers misinformation online: a false claim copied across hundreds of pages and reposted thousands of times doesn't become true, but it *feels* true, because you keep meeting it. We'll see this scaled to a flood in our very last episode — the "firehose of falsehood."

[GFX: a single false claim multiplying across a grid of fake "news" sites and social posts.]

**VO:** Repetition has a particularly nasty trick: even *fact-checking* can backfire if it repeats the false claim loudly. Hearing "Myth: X is true. Fact: it isn't" can leave some people remembering only "X," now feeling familiar. The familiarity outlives the correction.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Repetition]
- **Familiar ≠ true.** When something feels obviously right, ask: do I believe this because of *evidence*, or just because I've heard it a thousand times?
- **Count the repeats.** Are the same exact words coming from many mouths? That's a coordinated talking point, not a spontaneous consensus.
- **Trace it once.** For a claim you "just know," try to find where it actually comes from and whether it was ever checked.
- **Beware the catchy phrase.** The more sloganlike and sticky, the more it was built to bypass thought.

**VO:** The defense is to treat the feeling of familiarity as *neutral* — a sign you've heard something before, not a sign it's correct. Separate "I recognize this" from "this is true." They feel identical inside your head. They are not the same thing.

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, pick one "thing everybody knows" — a health tip, a historical "fact," a claim about a product or a group — that you've never actually checked, only absorbed. Spend ten minutes tracing it to a real source. Some will hold up. Some will dissolve into "people just keep saying it." Either way, you'll have caught your own brain mistaking familiarity for truth — the most important catch in this episode.

**VO:** Repetition makes you like things and believe things, through familiarity and fluency — even against your own knowledge. The cure: remember that recognizing a claim is not the same as verifying it.
