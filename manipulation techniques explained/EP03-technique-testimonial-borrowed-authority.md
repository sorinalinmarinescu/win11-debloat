# Episode 3 — Technique #2: Testimonial & Borrowed Authority
### Why a person in a white coat can sell you almost anything

**Block:** 1 · **Technique 2 of 20** · **Target runtime:** 12–14 min
**Anchor callback:** Campaign A — "20,679 physicians say Luckies are less irritating."
**Logline:** We outsource hard judgments to experts and celebrities. Manipulators rent that trust — sometimes from people with no real expertise at all.

**Learning objectives:**
- Tell a *legitimate* expert endorsement from a *borrowed* one.
- Understand the psychology of obedience to authority and why a costume is enough.
- Ask the three questions that pop a fake authority.

**VO:** For decades, cigarette companies put doctors in their ads. Real-looking doctors, in real-looking offices, calmly recommending a brand to soothe your throat. It sounds insane now. At the time, it sold billions of cigarettes.

[Beat.]

**VO:** The doctors weren't examining the science. They were renting out the one thing the company couldn't manufacture: your trust in a person who seems to know more than you.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Testimonial:** having a respected or famous figure vouch for a product, person, or idea.
**Borrowed authority:** borrowing the *appearance* of expertise — a title, a costume, a setting — to make a claim feel verified when it isn't.

**VO:** Testimonial is another of the classic 1937 propaganda devices. And it works because of a hard truth about modern life: nobody can be an expert in everything. You can't personally test every medicine, vet every economic plan, or audit every car's safety. So you take shortcuts. You trust doctors about health, scientists about science, and — strangely — famous people about almost anything.

[LOWER THIRD: "Testimonial" — Institute for Propaganda Analysis, 1937]

**VO:** That shortcut is usually sensible. The manipulation begins when the authority is *fake*, *irrelevant*, or *paid* — and you're not told which.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a brain with a little "trust" dial; an authority figure turns the dial up.]

**VO:** Psychologists have shown for decades that people defer to authority far more than they'd like to admit. In one famous and disturbing set of experiments in the 1960s, ordinary volunteers obeyed a man in a lab coat who told them to give what they believed were dangerous electric shocks to a stranger — simply because he seemed to be the authority in charge. The lesson wasn't that those people were monsters. It's that the *symbols* of authority — the coat, the clipboard, the confident voice — can switch off our own judgment.

[LOWER THIRD: On obedience to authority — Stanley Milgram experiments, 1960s]

**VO:** Robert Cialdini, who studies persuasion, lists "authority" as one of his core principles of influence. The unsettling part: we often respond to the *trappings* of authority — titles, uniforms, fancy offices — even when the person inside them knows no more than we do. The costume is doing the work.

[LOWER THIRD: Authority as a principle of persuasion — Robert Cialdini, *Influence*]

---

## SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign

[B-ROLL: the "20,679 physicians" Lucky Strike print ad.]

**VO:** Remember our cigarette campaign. One famous run of ads claimed that more than twenty thousand physicians said the brand was "less irritating." Look at the move. The exact number — twenty thousand, six hundred and seventy-nine — sounds precise and scientific. The white coat signals expertise. Your gut concludes: *the experts have checked this; it's safe.*

[LOWER THIRD: "20,679 physicians" ads, 1928–1932 · Source: Stanford (SRITA)]

**VO:** But the ad never tells you how those doctors were chosen, what they were asked, or whether anyone studied actual harm. The authority was real-looking and almost meaningless — borrowed, not earned. And it helped sell a product we now know kills its customers.

## SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today

[B-ROLL: montage — a toothpaste ad ("recommended by dentists"); a celebrity hawking a supplement; a "Dr." with a vague title on a talk show.]

**VO:** The costume changes; the trick doesn't.

**VO:** **Advertising.** "Nine out of ten dentists recommend" — recommend compared to what, chosen how? "Clinically proven" — proven by whom, in a study paid for by whom? An actor who *plays* a doctor on television gets hired to sell real pills, because the face is familiar and the white coat is close enough for your fast brain. Celebrity endorsements work the same way: we transfer a star's talent in one field — acting, sports — onto their opinion in a totally unrelated one, like skincare or cryptocurrency.

**VO:** **Politics.** Campaigns parade lists of generals, professors, or Nobel laureates who back their side. Sometimes those experts are genuinely relevant. Sometimes a physicist is being used to vouch for an economic policy, or a famous economist for a foreign-war decision — fields outside their expertise. The title is real; its *relevance* is the trick.

[GFX: a "title vs. relevance" meter — a big impressive title on the left, an "actually relevant?" needle that can swing to NO.]

**VO:** And watch for the vaguest costume of all: the word "experts." "Experts agree." Which experts? How many? Agreeing with what, exactly? "Experts" with no names is authority with the price tag torn off.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Borrowed Authority]
- **Named or nameless?** Real authority has a name you can check. "Studies show" and "experts say" are red flags until you can ask *who*.
- **Relevant or borrowed?** Is this person an expert in *this exact thing*? An Olympic sprinter is not an authority on vitamins.
- **Independent or paid?** Were they hired, gifted, or sponsored? A paid endorsement is an ad wearing a friend's face.
- **Evidence or vibes?** Does the authority point to checkable evidence, or just radiate confidence?

**VO:** None of this means "distrust all experts." That's the opposite error, and it's just as dangerous — we'll meet manufactured doubt later in the series. The skill is narrow: ask whether *this* authority is named, relevant, independent, and pointing at evidence. Real experts usually welcome those questions. Borrowed ones change the subject.

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, hunt for the phrase "studies show" or "experts say" — in ads, in news, in your feed. Each time, ask one question: *which* study, *which* expert? Try to actually find the source. Sometimes you'll find solid science. Sometimes you'll find a single survey paid for by the seller. Either way, you'll have done something most people never do: you'll have checked the costume for a real person inside.
