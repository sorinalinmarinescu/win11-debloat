# Episode 13 — Technique #11: Microtargeting & Psychographic Profiling
### Why your phone shows you a different message than your neighbor

**Block:** 2 · **Technique 11 of 20** · **Target runtime:** 13–14 min
**Anchor callback:** Campaign B — Cambridge Analytica's promise to sort voters by personality and message each one differently.
**Logline:** Microtargeting uses your data to aim a custom-built message at you alone — so each person sees the version most likely to move *them*, and no one sees the whole picture.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define *microtargeting* and *psychographic profiling* in plain terms.
- Understand why "a different message for everyone" is more dangerous than one shared lie.
- Keep the nuance: targeting is real and routine, but its mind-reading powers are often oversold.

---

## COLD OPEN

[B-ROLL: two neighbors on their porches, each looking at a phone; thought bubbles show two completely different political ads for the same candidate.]

**VO:** Two neighbors. Same street, same election, same candidate. One sees an ad about taxes and freedom. The other sees an ad — from the *same* campaign — about safety and fear. Neither knows the other got a different pitch. Neither can compare notes. Each thinks they've seen "the campaign."

[Beat.]

**VO:** That's microtargeting. And it quietly breaks something we used to take for granted: that we were all, more or less, looking at the same thing.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Microtargeting:** using data about individuals to deliver narrowly tailored messages to small, specific groups — or to single people.
**Psychographic profiling:** sorting people not just by age or location, but by *personality, values, and emotional triggers*.

**VO:** Old advertising was a megaphone: one message, blasted at everyone. Microtargeting is a thousand whispers, each tuned to a different ear. The advertiser starts with data — your age, your location, the pages you follow, the things you've clicked, bought, watched, and paused on — and uses it to guess what will move *you* specifically.

**VO:** Psychographics goes a step further, trying to sort you by personality: Are you anxious or adventurous? Do you respond to threats or to hope? The pitch of firms like the one in our Campaign B episode was that they could find the emotional key to each personality type and turn it.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a "mirror" that reflects back exactly what a person already fears or wants.]

**VO:** Microtargeting works for two reasons. The first is simple relevance: a message about your specific worry lands harder than a generic one. If a campaign knows you're worried about your job, an ad about jobs will hit you where you live.

**VO:** The second reason is the dangerous one, and it's about what *you* can't see. When everyone saw the same billboard, lies were riskier — someone would call them out, and the contradiction was public. Microtargeting removes the witnesses. A campaign can tell one group one thing and another group the opposite, and because the messages are private, the contradiction never surfaces. There's no town square anymore, just millions of separate rooms. Accountability depends on us seeing the same thing — and that's exactly what this technique dissolves.

**VO:** It also exploits a blind spot: we each assume our feed reflects reality, not a curated, personalized funhouse mirror built to reflect our own triggers back at us.

## SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign

[B-ROLL: the personality quiz; data streaming off profiles; "OCEAN" personality letters.]

**VO:** Cambridge Analytica is the poster child. Its sales pitch was pure psychographic microtargeting: harvest millions of profiles, score each person on personality traits, then serve each personality the message most likely to move it — the fearful get fear, the proud get pride.

[LOWER THIRD: Cambridge Analytica's psychographic pitch · Source: Campaign B episode]

**VO:** But remember our reality check. The careful evidence suggests the firm's mind-reading was more sales pitch than science — reportedly not even used as advertised in the campaign it's famous for, and judged by researchers to be roughly as good (or as limited) as ordinary targeting. So the lesson here is *two-sided*: microtargeting absolutely happens and is worth guarding against — *and* the specific fantasy of an algorithm that reads your soul and overrides your will is mostly hype. The everyday, boring version — "show job ads to worried workers" — is real and powerful enough.

---

## SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today

[B-ROLL: montage — an online store "recommended for you"; a political ad library; an insurance quote that changes by zip code.]

**VO:** **Advertising.** This is the engine of the modern internet. The reason an ad seems to "follow you around" after you looked at a product once is microtargeting. The reason two people get different prices for the same flight or insurance can be data-driven targeting. It's mostly mundane — and mostly invisible.

**VO:** **Politics.** Campaigns buy data on voters and serve different ads to different micro-audiences: one message to suburban parents, another to young renters, another to a single neighborhood. Done openly, some of this is just efficient outreach. Done in the dark, it lets a campaign be many contradictory things to many people, and to slip inflammatory messages to small groups where few will see and challenge them — the "dark ads" problem that pushed several countries to demand public ad libraries.

[GFX: a "shared billboard" (everyone sees it, it's accountable) vs. "a thousand private whispers" (no one can compare).]

**VO:** The healthy response isn't to panic that you're being mind-controlled. It's to remember that *what you see is a selection made for you* — and to go looking, sometimes, at what other people are being shown.

---

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Microtargeting]
- **"Why am I seeing this?"** Many platforms have an option that literally tells you why an ad was shown to you. Use it.
- **Assume a tailored version.** If it's an ad, someone chose to show it to *people like you*. Ask what version your opposite number is getting.
- **Compare feeds.** Talk to people unlike you about what ads and posts *they* see. The differences are revealing.
- **Resist the "soul-reading" panic.** Targeting nudges; it doesn't hypnotize. Don't trade naivety for helplessness.

**VO:** The deepest defense is to deliberately step outside your personalized bubble: read sources you wouldn't be shown, seek out the message aimed at the *other* group. You can't un-rig your own feed entirely, but you can refuse to mistake it for the whole world.

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, do two things. First, find the "why am I seeing this ad?" button on a platform you use and read what it says about how you've been categorized — it's often startling. Second, ask one person whose life differs from yours — a different age, place, or politics — to show you the ads and posts on *their* feed. Seeing how differently the same world is presented to the two of you is the whole lesson, learned in five minutes.

## RECAP + TEASE

**VO:** Microtargeting: a custom message for everyone, seen by no one else, so contradictions never get caught. Ask "why am I seeing this?" and go look at what others are shown. Real — but not mind control.
