# Episode 1 — Campaign A: "Torches of Freedom"
### How one man sold cigarettes to half a nation by selling them an idea

**Block:** 1 (Advertising & Public Relations) · **Type:** Example-campaign episode · **Target runtime:** 14–15 min
**Logline:** In 1929, a public-relations pioneer turned a cigarette into a symbol of women's freedom — without ever mentioning the cigarette. This is the campaign that teaches the whole first half of our course.

**Learning objectives — by the end the viewer can:**
- Tell the difference between *persuasion* (an open argument) and *manipulation* (influence that hides how it works).
- Recognize that a campaign can sell a **feeling or identity**, not a product.
- Name, at a glance, the ten techniques this campaign used (each gets its own episode next).

## COLD OPEN

[SFX: city sounds, 1920s — car horns, a brass band in the distance.]
[B-ROLL: black-and-white archival footage of New York's Fifth Avenue, Easter morning crowds in their best clothes.]

**VO:** New York City. Easter Sunday morning, the 31st of March, 1929. Fifth Avenue is packed for the Easter Parade. And then — a small commotion.

[B-ROLL: a group of well-dressed young women; one strikes a match; cigarettes lit; press photographers crowd in.]
[SFX: camera shutters, flashbulbs popping.]

**VO:** A young woman named Bertha Hunt stops, lifts a cigarette to her lips, and lights it — in public. Then another woman does the same. And another. Photographers, who happen to be perfectly positioned, capture every second.

[GFX: a 1929-style newspaper front page assembles on screen. Headline animates in: "WOMEN LIGHT 'TORCHES OF FREEDOM'".]

**VO:** By the next morning, newspapers across the country carry the image and the phrase the women used: they weren't smoking cigarettes — they were lighting *torches of freedom*. A blow against male oppression. A symbol of equality.

[Beat. Music drops out.]

**VO:** There was just one thing the newspapers didn't print. The march wasn't a spontaneous protest. It was an advertisement. And the man who built it would help invent the century we still live in.

## SEGMENT 1 — Meet the engineer of consent

[B-ROLL: portrait of Edward Bernays; archival images of 1920s advertising offices.]
[LOWER THIRD: Edward Bernays (1891–1995) · "the father of public relations"]

**VO:** The man behind the march was Edward Bernays. History often calls him "the father of public relations." He was also the nephew of Sigmund Freud, the founder of psychoanalysis — and that family connection is the key to everything he did.

**VO:** Freud studied the hidden, unconscious drives beneath human behavior. Bernays asked a commercial question: if people are driven by desires they don't fully understand, then you don't sell them a product by listing its features. You sell them the *desire* — and let them think the choice was their own.

[GFX: a simple two-box animation. Box 1: "Old advertising — 'Our soap is 99% pure.'" Box 2: "Bernays — 'Buy this and become the person you want to be.'"]

**VO:** Bernays wrote this down. In his 1928 book, plainly titled *Propaganda*, he argued that the deliberate shaping of public opinion was a normal, even necessary, part of modern democracy — and that a small number of skilled people would do the shaping. He didn't think this was sinister. He thought it was *engineering*. He even called it "the engineering of consent."

[LOWER THIRD: Edward Bernays, *Propaganda* (1928) · *Crystallizing Public Opinion* (1923)]

**VO:** Keep that idea in your pocket for the whole course. The most powerful persuasion doesn't feel like persuasion at all. It feels like your own free choice.

## SEGMENT 2 — The problem on the table

[B-ROLL: 1920s street scenes; men smoking everywhere; women conspicuously not.]

**VO:** In the 1920s, the American Tobacco Company had a problem its president, George Washington Hill, was desperate to solve. Men smoked. Women, by and large, did not — at least not in public. In many places it was a social taboo; a woman lighting up on the street could be scolded, even stopped by police.

[GFX: a pie chart of "the market," with one half greyed out and labeled "half the adult population — not buying."]

**VO:** Hill looked at that taboo and saw, in his words, a gold mine in front of his company. Half the adult population was off-limits. If smoking could become acceptable — even admirable — for women, he could nearly double his market. He hired Bernays to make it happen.

**VO:** Notice what the real goal was. Not "tell women facts about cigarettes." The goal was to **change what the act of smoking *meant*** — to move it from "shameful" to "liberating." That is a much deeper kind of influence, and it's why this campaign is our first lesson.

---

## SEGMENT 3 — The campaign in action: how it was targeted

[GFX: section header — "THE PLAN."]

**VO:** Here's how a master did it. Watch the targeting, because the targeting is the genius.

**VO:** **Step one: find the real obstacle.** Bernays didn't guess. He consulted a psychoanalyst named Abraham Brill to understand *why* women resisted public smoking and what might move them. Brill's insight: for women pushing against a male-dominated world, a cigarette could stand for something — independence, defiance, freedom. Cigarettes, he suggested, could be "torches of freedom."

[LOWER THIRD: Bernays consulted psychoanalyst A. A. Brill · Source: Wikipedia, "PR campaigns of Edward Bernays"]

**VO:** **Step two: attach the product to a powerful idea.** The 1920s women's movement was alive and visible; women had just won the vote in 1920. Bernays didn't argue that cigarettes were good. He quietly welded the cigarette onto a cause people already believed in: women's equality. The product borrowed the glow of the cause.

[GFX: animation — a plain cigarette slides next to the word "FREEDOM"; a glow transfers from the word to the cigarette.]

**VO:** **Step three: stage an event the press can't ignore.** Bernays arranged for a group of fashionable young women — including his own secretary, Bertha Hunt — to march in the Easter Parade and light cigarettes on cue, in front of photographers he'd made sure would be there. The women were given precise instructions about when and how to light up. To the public, it looked like news: women spontaneously striking a blow for equality. In reality, it was choreography.

[LOWER THIRD: Easter Sunday Parade, NYC, 31 March 1929 · Sources: Wikipedia; The New Yorker (1998); American Historical Association]

**VO:** **Step four: let other voices carry the message.** Bernays didn't want American Tobacco's name anywhere near it. The messengers were the women themselves, the photographers, the newspapers, and — crucially — the *idea*. When the message seems to come from everywhere except the seller, we drop our guard.

[GFX: a network diagram — "American Tobacco" sits in the center but greyed out; arrows of influence flow out through "young women," "press," "feminism," "the public."]

**VO:** It worked. The stunt became national news. And it was only the opening move.

## SEGMENT 4 — Doubling down: the rest of the playbook

[B-ROLL: archival Lucky Strike print ads — doctors in white coats; slim women; "It's toasted."]

**VO:** Over the next few years, the campaign for Lucky Strike piled technique on technique.

**VO:** They ran ads in which doctors vouched for the brand — one famous line claimed that more than twenty thousand physicians said Luckies were "less irritating." The number sounds scientific. The message to your gut was simpler: *people in white coats approve, so it must be safe.*

[LOWER THIRD: "20,679 physicians" ads ran 1928–1932 · Source: Stanford (SRITA)]

**VO:** They told women to "reach for a Lucky instead of a sweet" — linking smoking to staying slim, tapping straight into anxiety about weight and beauty. And they repeated a single hypnotic slogan — "It's toasted" — until it lodged in the national ear, whether or not anyone knew what it meant.

[GFX: kinetic typography — "REACH FOR A LUCKY INSTEAD OF A SWEET" and "IT'S TOASTED" pulse on screen.]

**VO:** And then there's my favorite, because it shows how far this kind of thinking can go. When surveys suggested some women avoided the Lucky Strike pack because its green color clashed with their clothes, Bernays didn't change the package. He set out to change *fashion itself* — promoting the color green through events and tastemakers so that the world would rearrange itself around the product.

[LOWER THIRD: The "green" campaign, c. 1934 · Source: New York Times obituary of Bernays]

**VO:** Read that again. Rather than persuade you to want the thing, he changed your surroundings so the thing already fit. That's not advertising a product. That's redecorating reality.

**VO:** The numbers tell the rest. One brand's sales climbed from around 14 billion cigarettes in 1925 to 40 billion by 1930. A taboo had been turned inside out.

[GFX: animated bar chart climbing from 14B (1925) to 40B (1930). Source caption.]
[LOWER THIRD: Sales figures · Source: Wikipedia, "Lucky Strike"]

## SEGMENT 5 — An honest word: don't overstate it

[GFX: a small "REALITY CHECK" stamp.]

**VO:** Now, a habit we'll keep all series: don't believe the legend just because it's a good story. Some historians point out that Bernays loved to take credit, and that broad social changes — women working, voting, claiming public space — were already underway. One march did not single-handedly remake a country, and Bernays may have polished his own role over the years.

[LOWER THIRD: See scholarship questioning the Bernays legend · Source: Journalism History (2014)]

**VO:** That caution *is* the lesson. Manipulators — and the people who write about them — often exaggerate how powerful their tools are. A campaign can be real, clever, and effective, *and* the hype around it can still be inflated. Holding both ideas at once is exactly the kind of careful thinking this course is trying to build.

## SEGMENT 6 — The ten techniques (your roadmap)

[GFX: a 10-item "season map" builds, one line at a time, each with its episode number.]

**VO:** So here's where we're going. Everything Bernays did breaks down into ten repeatable techniques — and each one gets its own short episode next. Here is your map.

[ON SCREEN: numbered list, revealed line by line as narrated.]

**VO:**
1. **Transfer** — gluing a product to a feeling you already have, like freedom.
2. **Testimonial and borrowed authority** — the doctors in white coats.
3. **Bandwagon and social proof** — the sense that everyone's doing it.
4. **Pseudo-events** — staging something just so it gets reported as news.
5. **The third-party technique** — hiding the seller behind "independent" voices.
6. **Glittering generalities** — shiny virtue-words like "freedom" that mean everything and nothing.
7. **Engineering emotion and desire** — selling an identity, and poking fears like the fear of being fat.
8. **Framing** — controlling what an act *means* before you can decide.
9. **Repetition** — saying it until it feels true, like "It's toasted."
10. **Engineering the environment** — changing the world around you so the choice feels natural, like the green campaign.

**VO:** Learn these ten, and you've learned the grammar of modern advertising — and a good chunk of modern politics, too.

## SEGMENT 7 — Your first tool: the five questions

[GFX: the recurring "SPOT-IT" card.]

**VO:** Before the next episode, install one habit. Whenever a message is clearly trying to move you, ask five questions:

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST]
1. **Who's talking — and who's paying?**
2. **What am I being made to *feel* right now?**
3. **What do they want me to *do* while I feel it?**
4. **What's missing — the other side, the price, the source?**
5. **Would this still convince me tomorrow, calm, in daylight?**

**VO:** Run the Torches of Freedom march through those questions and the whole machine lights up. Who's paying? A cigarette company. What's the feeling? Pride, defiance, belonging. What do they want? For you to buy and to smoke in public. What's missing? Any mention of the seller — or the health of your lungs.
