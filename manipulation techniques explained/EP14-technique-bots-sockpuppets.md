# Episode 14 — Technique #12: Bots, Sockpuppets & Manufactured Crowds
### How a handful of operators fake a roaring mob

**Block:** 2 · **Technique 12 of 20** · **Target runtime:** 13–14 min
**Anchor callback:** Campaign B — the troll farm where paid workers ran armies of fake "American" accounts.
**Logline:** This is astroturfing for the digital age: fake accounts — automated bots and human-run "sockpuppets" — impersonate ordinary people to fake a crowd, hijack trends, and make a fringe view look like the popular will.

**Learning objectives:**
- Tell apart *bots* (automated) and *sockpuppets* (fake personas run by people).
- Understand why a fake crowd works even when you suspect some accounts are fake.
- Spot the tells of an inauthentic account or coordinated campaign.

**VO:** Picture one person at one desk, running fifty different accounts. Fifty names, fifty profile photos, fifty "personalities" — a worried mom, a proud veteran, a fired-up student. Now picture a building full of those people. Now picture software that runs not fifty but fifty *thousand* accounts automatically.

[Beat.]

**VO:** That's how you manufacture a crowd out of almost no one. And a crowd, as we learned back in Block 1, is one of the most persuasive things on earth.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Bot:** an automated account that posts, likes, and shares by software, not a person.
**Sockpuppet:** a fake identity operated by a real person to hide who's really talking.
**Coordinated inauthentic behavior:** many fake accounts acting together to fake a grassroots movement.

**VO:** Remember astroturfing from Block 1 — fake grassroots, the front group in a friendly mask? Bots and sockpuppets are astroturfing turned into an industrial machine. Instead of one fake "citizens' group," you create thousands of fake *citizens*, each looking like an individual, all secretly working for the same hidden hand.

**VO:** The goal is to fake three things: **numbers** (make a view look popular), **consensus** (make it look like "everyone" agrees), and **momentum** (push something to "trend" so the algorithms — and then real people — amplify it).

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: the "everyone is staring at the sky" crowd from Episode 4 returns — but now half the figures are revealed as cardboard cutouts.]

**VO:** This technique is a parasite on the social-proof instinct from Episode 4. Your brain reads "lots of people are saying this" as "this must be true and normal." Bots and sockpuppets feed that instinct junk food: a manufactured "lots of people."

**VO:** Here's the cruel twist. It works *even if you doubt some accounts are fake.* You might scroll past an angry thread thinking "half of these are probably bots" — but the sheer volume still shifts your sense of where "most people" stand. It makes a fringe position feel mainstream and a mainstream position feel embattled. And it bullies the social-proof of silence: when a topic looks swarmed by one side, real people with other views go quiet, which makes the fake majority look even more real.

**VO:** There's also a sneaky second job. Some fake accounts don't push a view at all; they just pollute. They flood every conversation with noise, insults, and nonsense until honest people give up and leave. Exhaustion is a goal, not a side effect.

## SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign

[B-ROLL: the troll farm; fake "American" profile pages; a staged online "movement."]

**VO:** Our Campaign B troll farm was a sockpuppet factory. Investigators documented paid employees running large numbers of fake accounts posing as ordinary Americans across the political spectrum — building pages, growing followers, and even using those fake personas to organize real-world events. The accounts looked homegrown. The hand behind them was foreign and coordinated.

[LOWER THIRD: Coordinated fake accounts run by the Internet Research Agency · Source: bipartisan Senate Intelligence Committee reports]

**VO:** Crucially, the point wasn't one message. It was to *be* the crowd on every side — to manufacture the appearance of furious, mobilized masses where there were really just shift workers and scripts. A fake crowd, pointed at a real one.

## SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today

[B-ROLL: montage — a product with thousands of suspiciously similar 5-star reviews; a hashtag "trending" from a tiny cluster of accounts; identical replies under a post.]

**VO:** **Advertising and reputation.** Fake reviews and fake followers are a paid service. A brand-new product can buy a thousand glowing reviews; an "influencer" can buy a following; a company can flood a rival's page with complaints. The crowd you're reading may be for hire.

**VO:** **Politics and public debate.** Bot and sockpuppet networks push hashtags to fake "trends," swarm opponents, and make tiny factions look like national movements. They're used by states, by campaigns, by activist groups, by anyone who wants to look bigger than they are. After 2016, platforms began removing networks of fake accounts by the millions and labeling it "coordinated inauthentic behavior" — an admission of just how routine it had become.

[GFX: a "trending" topic zooms out to reveal it was driven by a small cluster of accounts each posting hundreds of times.]

**VO:** None of this means everyone who disagrees with you is a bot — that accusation is itself a manipulation, a lazy way to dismiss real people. The skill is narrower: learn to ask whether a *suspiciously sudden, suspiciously uniform* surge of "public opinion" is actually a manufactured crowd.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Manufactured Crowds]
- **Check the account, not just the post.** Brand-new? No real history? A stock or stolen photo? A name like a word plus eight digits? Posts at all hours, nonstop? Tells of a bot.
- **Look for uniformity.** Many accounts posting near-identical wording at near-identical times is coordination, not coincidence.
- **Sudden surges.** A topic that goes from nowhere to "everywhere" in an hour deserves suspicion, not reflex sharing.
- **Volume vs. variety.** A real movement has messy, varied voices. A fake one repeats a few scripts.

**VO:** And the master move: judge ideas by *evidence*, not by *crowd size*. If you never let "lots of accounts agree" count as proof in the first place, you've already disarmed the entire technique — bots included.

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, next time something is "trending" or a post has a swarm of identical replies, spend two minutes clicking into a few of the accounts. When was it created? What else does it post? Is there a real human history, or just a flag emoji, a slogan, and a wall of rage? You won't always be sure — and that's fine. The goal is just to break the automatic reflex of treating a loud crowd as a real one.
