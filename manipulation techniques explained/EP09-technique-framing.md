# Episode 9 — Technique #8: Framing
### The same fact, two frames, two different decisions

**Block:** 1 · **Technique 8 of 20** · **Target runtime:** 13–14 min
**Anchor callback:** Campaign A — reframing smoking from "shameful" to "liberating."
**Logline:** Framing is choosing the words, the comparison, and the angle that decide what a fact *means* — and quietly decide it for you, before you weigh anything.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define *framing* and see, with a clean experiment, that wording changes choices.
- Recognize the main framing moves: gain vs. loss, the chosen baseline, what's named and what's hidden.
- Practice "reframing" any claim to see the choice clearly.

---

## COLD OPEN

[GFX: two surgeons, two waiting rooms. Surgeon A: "This operation has a 90% survival rate." Surgeon B: "This operation has a 10% death rate."]

**VO:** Two surgeons describe the exact same operation. One says it has a ninety percent survival rate. The other says it has a ten percent death rate. Identical facts. Which surgeon would you choose?

[Beat.]

**VO:** Most people pick the first — and feel calmer about the surgery — even though the two sentences are mathematically the same. That gap, between identical facts and opposite feelings, is the whole game today.

[GFX: TITLE CARD — "Technique #8: FRAMING."]

---

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Framing:** presenting the same information in a way — through wording, comparison, or emphasis — that steers how people feel about it and what they decide.

**VO:** A frame is like the edge of a photograph. The photographer chooses what's inside the frame and what's cropped out, and that choice shapes the whole picture — without altering a single thing that's actually there. Framing does the same with information: it chooses the words, the comparison, the baseline, and what to leave out.

**VO:** This isn't just folk wisdom. Psychologists Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman demonstrated it in a famous 1981 study. They gave people a choice about fighting a disease, described once in terms of how many lives would be *saved* and once in terms of how many would be *lost* — same numbers, same outcomes. People's choices flipped depending only on the wording. The frame, not the facts, drove the decision.

[LOWER THIRD: Framing effect — Tversky & Kahneman, 1981 ("Asian disease problem")]

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a single number "5%" shown two ways — "95% fat-free!" (appealing) vs. "contains 5% fat" (less appealing).]

**VO:** Your brain doesn't process raw facts; it processes facts *as presented*. We're especially sensitive to gains versus losses: research shows we feel the pain of a loss more strongly than the pleasure of an equal gain. So "don't lose this deal" hits harder than "get this deal," and "protect what you have" hits harder than "you could gain." Manipulators frame in terms of loss when they want you anxious and acting fast.

**VO:** Frames also work by setting the *baseline* — the thing you compare to. A price feels cheap next to a higher "original" price (which may have been invented for exactly that comparison). A number feels huge or tiny depending on what it's placed beside. Change the comparison and you change the meaning, without changing the number.

**VO:** And frames work by *naming.* Calling a payment a "fee" or a "surcharge" or an "investment" or a "contribution" steers your feeling about identical dollars. Calling people "rebels" or "terrorists," a policy a "reform" or a "cut" — same thing in the world, opposite reaction in your head. Whoever names it, frames it.

---

## SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign

[B-ROLL: the shift from "smoking is unladylike" to "smoking is liberating."]

**VO:** Our whole cigarette campaign was, at its heart, a giant reframing. Before Bernays, the public frame for a woman smoking in the street was "improper," even "shameful." He didn't argue against that frame head-on. He built a new one and made it more attractive: smoking as *freedom*, as *equality*, as *modern*. Same act — woman, cigarette, street — wrapped in a new frame, producing the opposite feeling.

**VO:** That's framing at industrial scale. He didn't change what smoking *was*. He changed what it *meant*. And meaning is what we actually decide on.

---

## SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today

[B-ROLL: montage — "95% fat-free"; "was $200, now $99"; news chyrons describing the same event with opposite words.]

**VO:** **Advertising.** "Ninety-five percent fat-free" sells; "five percent fat" doesn't — same yogurt. "Was two hundred dollars, now ninety-nine" frames a price as a rescue from loss. "Only a dollar a day" reframes a big annual cost into a small daily one. Each is true; each is engineered to feel a certain way.

**VO:** **Politics.** Framing is the daily weather of political language. Is it an "estate tax" or a "death tax"? "Undocumented immigrants" or "illegal aliens"? A "government spending program" or an "investment in our future"? A military action with "collateral damage" or with "civilian deaths"? The underlying reality is the same; the chosen words pre-load your verdict. Whole think tanks exist to test which frame moves people, then flood the airwaves with it.

[GFX: a "same event, two chyrons" demo — a single photo with two opposite news captions.]

**VO:** Watch, too, for the *missing* frame — what got cropped out. A chart that starts its scale at a flattering number to make a small change look enormous. A statistic with no comparison, so you can't tell if it's big or small. The crop is a choice, and the choice is an argument.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Framing]
- **Flip it.** Restate the claim the opposite way (survival ↔ death, fat-free ↔ contains fat). Does your feeling change while the facts stay put? That's the frame talking.
- **Find the baseline.** What is this being compared to — and did someone choose that comparison to flatter the result?
- **Check the words.** Swap loaded labels for neutral ones. "Death tax" → "tax on large inheritances." Does it still land the same?
- **Ask what's cropped.** What's just outside the frame — the other side, the cost, the missing comparison?

**VO:** The master skill here is *reframing*: deliberately re-describe any claim in two or three different ways. When you can state something as a gain, a loss, and a neutral fact, you stop being a passenger in someone else's frame and start steering your own.

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, catch one number or one headline and reframe it three ways: once to make it sound great, once to make it sound terrible, once flat and neutral. A "20% off" sale: great ("save twenty percent!"), terrible ("you're still paying eighty percent for something you didn't plan to buy"), neutral ("the price is X"). Doing this a few times rewires you to feel the frame as a *choice* someone made — which is the first step to making your own.

**VO:** Framing: same facts, different words, different decision. Flip it, find the baseline, check the loaded words, and ask what's cropped out. Whoever names it, frames it — so learn to rename it yourself.
