# Episode 6 — Technique #5: The Third-Party Technique & Front Groups
### How sellers hide behind voices that seem independent

**Block:** 1 · **Technique 5 of 20** · **Target runtime:** 13–14 min
**Anchor callback:** Campaign A — Bernays kept the tobacco company's name out of it and let "independent" voices carry the message.
**Logline:** We trust a message more when it doesn't come from someone trying to sell us something. So manipulators launder their message through a third party — a "neutral" expert, a "grassroots" group, a "regular person" who's secretly on the payroll.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define the *third-party technique* and *front groups*, and meet the word *astroturfing*.
- Understand why a hidden source is more persuasive than an open one.
- Learn to trace a message back to who benefits.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Third-party technique:** delivering your message through someone who appears independent, so it doesn't look like you're promoting yourself.
**Front group / astroturf:** an organization secretly created or funded by an interested party to look like a spontaneous, grassroots movement.

**VO:** Edward Bernays openly taught the third-party technique. His rule: if you want to sell something, don't say it yourself — get a seemingly disinterested authority or group to say it for you. The audience trusts the *messenger's* independence, never suspecting the message was written by the seller.

**VO:** When that "independent" group is actually a costume for a corporation or political interest, we call it *astroturfing* — fake grassroots. The term is credited to U.S. Senator Lloyd Bentsen in 1985, who said he could tell the difference between real "grass roots" and "AstroTurf," the brand of fake stadium grass, after being buried in suspiciously identical letters orchestrated by insurance companies.

[LOWER THIRD: "Astroturf" coined by Sen. Lloyd Bentsen, 1985 · Source: Merriam-Webster]

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: two speech bubbles. One labeled "the seller" (you discount it). One labeled "an independent voice" (you trust it). The seller secretly holds a puppet string on the second.]

**VO:** You already run a built-in discount on anything that comes from a salesperson. When a company says "our product is the best," you mentally subtract — *well, they would say that.* This is healthy skepticism.

**VO:** The third-party technique exists to dodge that discount. If the same claim comes from a "neutral expert," a "concerned citizens' group," or a stranger who seems to have nothing to gain, the discount doesn't fire. You take it at face value. The persuasion sneaks in through the one door you left unlocked: your trust in people who *aren't* selling.

**VO:** This is why hidden sources are so powerful — and why the most important question in this whole course might be a two-word Latin phrase: *cui bono?* Who benefits? Manipulators work hard to make sure that question never crosses your mind.

---

## SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign

[B-ROLL: the Torches march; newspaper coverage; fashion editors.]

**VO:** Bernays was a master of this. In the Torches of Freedom march, the tobacco company's name appeared nowhere. The messengers were young women "spontaneously" claiming their freedom, and the newspapers that reported it. For the green-color campaign, he worked through fashion editors, charity balls, and tastemakers — independent-seeming voices who made green fashionable without announcing they were doing a cigarette company's bidding.

**VO:** In each case, the public heard the message from someone they had no reason to distrust. The seller was the puppeteer, standing offstage.

## SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today

[B-ROLL: montage — an "independent review" site; a "patient advocacy" group; "influencers" with tiny "#ad" tags; identical letters to a newspaper.]

**VO:** This technique may be the most widespread in this entire series, because the internet made it cheap.

**VO:** **Advertising.** The "independent review site" that's secretly owned by the company it praises. The social-media influencer who frames a paid promotion as a personal favorite — sometimes with a tiny "ad" label, sometimes with none. The "user" in the comments raving about a product, who works for the company. Each one is a third party renting you their trustworthiness.

**VO:** **Politics and public affairs.** Industry-funded "institutes" and "coalitions" with neutral, public-spirited names, created to argue an industry's position while looking like concerned citizens or independent scholars. Tobacco and other industries have funded groups to manufacture doubt about scientific findings, precisely so the doubt seems to come from neutral experts rather than the companies with money on the line. Online, "grassroots" campaigns can be coordinated from a single office — a preview of the bot and sockpuppet armies we'll cover in Block 2.

[GFX: a "follow the money" animation — a friendly front-group logo is lifted like a mask to reveal a corporate or political funder behind it.]

**VO:** The tell is almost always hidden funding. A genuine grassroots group is usually happy to tell you who pays for it. A front group buries it.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Hidden Sources]
- **Cui bono — who benefits?** If this message is true and you act on it, who makes money or gains power?
- **Who funds the messenger?** Look for the "About," "Funding," or "Sponsors" page. Vague or missing? Suspicious.
- **Does the "independent" voice always land on one side?** A truly neutral group sometimes disagrees with its funders. A front group never does.
- **Is this a real person?** Online, the "regular customer" or "ordinary voter" may be an account built for the job.

**VO:** The defense is to treat *every* message as if it has a hidden author until proven otherwise, and to ask who gains. You're not becoming paranoid — you're just refusing to let people skip the salesperson discount by wearing a disguise.

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, pick one "advocacy group," "institute," or "coalition" you see cited in the news, and spend five minutes finding out who funds it. Search the name plus the word "funding" or "donors." Sometimes you'll find an open, honest nonprofit. Sometimes you'll find a single industry writing the checks. The search itself is the muscle you're building.
