# Episode 18 — Technique #16: Name-Calling & Demonization
### Attacking the person to avoid the argument

**Block:** 2 · **Technique 16 of 20** · **Target runtime:** 12–13 min
**Anchor callback:** Campaign B — portraying opposing groups as contemptible enemies.
**Logline:** When you can't beat an argument, beat up the person making it. Name-calling glues a damning label onto an opponent or idea so the audience rejects it without ever examining it.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define *name-calling* and the *ad hominem* fallacy, and tell them from fair criticism.
- See why a sticky negative label sticks.
- Learn to separate the messenger from the message.

---

## COLD OPEN

[GFX: a debate stage. One speaker opens their mouth to make a point; the other simply says a single sneering label, and the crowd jeers. The point is never heard.]

**VO:** Here's a magic trick that requires no facts at all. Your opponent makes a strong argument. Instead of answering it, you give them a nasty nickname — and you repeat it, and repeat it. Soon, nobody remembers the argument. They just remember the insult. You've "won" without engaging a single idea.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Name-calling:** attaching a negative label to a person, group, or idea to make the audience reject it without examination.
**Ad hominem:** attacking the person making an argument instead of the argument itself ("to the man").

**VO:** Name-calling is the very first item on that classic 1937 list of propaganda devices — it's the dark twin of the glittering generalities we met in Block 1. Where a glittering generality glues a *good* word onto something so you'll accept it, name-calling glues a *bad* word onto something so you'll reject it. "Radical." "Traitor." "Sheep." "Crook." "Extremist." "Puppet." The label does the work that evidence is supposed to do.

[LOWER THIRD: "Name-calling" — Institute for Propaganda Analysis, 1937]

**VO:** Its logical cousin is the ad hominem fallacy: dodging someone's point by attacking *them*. Note the crucial difference from fair criticism. Pointing out that a paid lobbyist has a conflict of interest is relevant. Calling them a "loser" to avoid their actual evidence is name-calling. One informs your judgment; the other replaces it.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a clean idea with a dirty label slapped on it; the whole thing now looks dirty.]

**VO:** Two quirks make this work. First, the "halo" in reverse: once we feel something is *bad*, that feeling spreads to everything attached to it. Tar the speaker, and the speech looks tainted too — even if it's true. We struggle to separate "I dislike this person" from "this person is wrong."

**VO:** Second, repetition — straight from our illusory-truth episode. A nickname repeated a thousand times becomes the way you *think* of the person, automatically, before any reasoning starts. That's why operators choose short, sticky, sneering labels and hammer them relentlessly. They're not trying to win a debate; they're installing a reflex.

**VO:** And name-calling is contagious in a way arguments aren't. It gives your own side a fun, easy weapon — chanting the insult feels like belonging, like winning. So it spreads on its own, and the actual question quietly disappears.

---

## SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign

[B-ROLL: the troll farm; memes mocking and vilifying every side.]

**VO:** Our Campaign B operation leaned hard on this. A great deal of its content wasn't argument at all — it was mockery and vilification, painting opponents of every stripe as stupid, evil, or subhuman. Name-calling pairs naturally with the us-vs-them and outrage machines: the insult marks who's "them," and the contempt feels good to share. You don't fact-check a sneer. You just laugh, or rage, and pass it on.

---

## SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today

[B-ROLL: montage — attack ads; pundits trading insults; comment sections; a brand mocking a competitor.]

**VO:** **Advertising.** "Comparative" ads that don't compare features but mock the rival's customers as fools. The whole genre of making the *other* product's user look pathetic.

**VO:** **Politics.** This may be the most visible technique in modern politics. Attack ads that brand an opponent with a single damning word. Nicknames designed to belittle. Whole media ecosystems built around contempt for the other side — where the point of a segment isn't to make an argument but to make the audience feel superior. Notice how rarely the *strongest* version of the opponent's actual position gets quoted. That's the tell: name-calling thrives precisely where engagement with the real argument is missing.

[GFX: a "respond to the strongest version" vs. "mock the person" fork.]

**VO:** Watch, too, for the reverse — the *manufactured slur*, where a normal word is redefined as an insult so that anyone using it can be dismissed, or where any criticism is pre-labeled as some "-ism" or "-phobia" or "-ist" to avoid answering it. Both sides of any fight do this. The structure is identical: replace the argument with a label.

---

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Name-Calling]
- **Label vs. evidence.** Is the attack on the *idea* (with reasons) or on the *person* (with insults)?
- **Would the point survive a nicer messenger?** If a calm, likable person made the same argument, would it still be dismissed? If yes, the insult is a dodge.
- **Is the strongest version being answered?** Manipulators attack a caricature; honest debaters engage the best case.
- **Relevant flaw or just a sneer?** A genuine conflict of interest matters. "They're a clown" doesn't.

**VO:** The defense is a discipline: separate the messenger from the message. You can dislike a person and still owe their argument an honest hearing. Ask, "Setting aside how I feel about who's saying it — is the point true?" That single separation defuses most name-calling on the spot.

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, find an argument you agree with that's being made mostly through insults aimed at the other side — yes, *your* side doing the name-calling. Try to restate your own position using only evidence, with zero insults. It's harder than it sounds, and it's the best workout there is. If your view is strong, it survives without the sneer. If it can only be defended with mockery, that's worth knowing too.

**VO:** Name-calling and demonization: destroy the person to avoid the point, with sticky labels and repetition. Separate the messenger from the message, and ask whether the strongest version is being answered.
