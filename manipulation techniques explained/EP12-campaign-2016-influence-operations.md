# Episode 12 — Campaign B: The 2016-Era Influence Operations
### How manipulation moved from the street into your phone

**Block:** 2 (Politics & Digital Influence) · **Type:** Example-campaign episode · **Target runtime:** 14–15 min
**Logline:** Almost a century after the Torches of Freedom march, the same human weaknesses were targeted again — but now one at a time, in private, by software. We follow two documented operations from the 2016 era and use them to map the next ten techniques.

**Learning objectives — by the end the viewer can:**
- Describe how digital influence differs from old mass advertising: targeted, private, automated, and hard to see.
- Understand two real, documented operations — a state "troll farm" and a data-profiling firm.
- Hold the crucial nuance: these tools are real *and* their power is often overstated.
- Name the ten techniques the rest of the course will explain.

## SEGMENT 1 — What changed since the cigarettes

[GFX: split timeline — 1929 "one message → millions" vs. 2016 "millions of messages → one person each."]

**VO:** Edward Bernays had to put one message in front of millions of people through newspapers everybody read. Crude, but visible — you could see the same ad your neighbor saw.

**VO:** The modern toolkit flips that. Three things changed everything. First, **data**: companies now hold thousands of details about you — what you like, click, watch, and linger on. Second, **targeting**: a message can be aimed at just *you*, or just people like you, and shown to no one else. Third, **automation**: software, not people, decides what billions of us see, and fake accounts can imitate a crowd at the press of a button.

**VO:** The result is persuasion you mostly can't see. There's no shared public ad to point at and argue about. The manipulation happens in private, in your pocket, tuned to your weaknesses. To learn how it works, we'll look at two operations from around 2016 that were investigated in extraordinary public detail.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Operation one: the troll farm

[B-ROLL: a nondescript office building; rows of computers; shift workers.]
[LOWER THIRD: The "Internet Research Agency," St. Petersburg, Russia]

**VO:** In St. Petersburg, Russia, an organization called the Internet Research Agency ran what investigators described as a "troll farm" — a building full of paid employees whose job was to pretend to be other people online. In 2018, the U.S. Special Counsel Robert Mueller formally accused thirteen Russian individuals and three organizations of a scheme to interfere in American politics.

[LOWER THIRD: Mueller indictment, 16 Feb 2018 · Source: U.S. House Intelligence Committee]

**VO:** Here's the part that matters most for our purposes, and it's confirmed in *bipartisan* U.S. Senate reports — backed by both major parties. The stated goal wasn't to push one tidy message. It was to **sow discord** — to widen the cracks already in society.

[LOWER THIRD: Bipartisan Senate Intelligence Committee reports, Dec 2018]

**VO:** So the operation played *every* side at once. It ran accounts and pages posing as angry partisans on the right *and* as activists on the left. It built pages targeting Black Americans, pages targeting religious conservatives, pages for immigrants and against immigration — sometimes organizing real protesters on opposite sides of the same street, all from the same office. The aim wasn't to win an argument. It was to make Americans hate each other more.

[GFX: a single hidden hand puppeteering two angry crowds facing off; both strings lead back to one desk.]

**VO:** Sit with how different that is from a normal campaign. A normal campaign wants you to agree with it. This operation didn't care what you believed — only that you believed it *furiously*, and trusted your fellow citizens a little less.

---

## SEGMENT 3 — Operation two: the profiling firm

[B-ROLL: a personality quiz on a phone; data points streaming off a Facebook profile.]
[LOWER THIRD: Cambridge Analytica]

**VO:** The second operation is about *data*. A political consulting firm called Cambridge Analytica became the symbol of a new fear: that your social-media life could be mined to manipulate your vote.

**VO:** It worked, roughly, like this. A researcher built a personality quiz app. The people who took it didn't just hand over their own answers — through the social network's settings at the time, the app also scooped up data on their *friends*, who never agreed to anything. By this route, the data of up to eighty-seven million people was harvested. The firm's pitch was that it could sort voters by personality type and hit each with tailor-made messages — what's called "psychographic" targeting.

[LOWER THIRD: Up to 87 million profiles harvested · Whistleblower: Christopher Wylie, 2018]

**VO:** The firm worked for political campaigns, and when a whistleblower named Christopher Wylie went public in 2018, it became a global scandal about privacy and manipulation.

---

## SEGMENT 4 — The most important lesson: don't believe the hype

[GFX: a big "REALITY CHECK" stamp.]

**VO:** Now stop — because here is the single most valuable thing in this episode, and it's the opposite of what the headlines screamed.

**VO:** The scary story was that a magic algorithm read voters' souls and flipped an election. The careful evidence says: probably not. Reporting later noted that the firm itself admitted the fancy "psychographic" method wasn't actually used in the campaign people most associate it with. Britain's privacy regulator, after investigating, found the firm's tools weren't the world-beating weapon advertised, and found no evidence it misused data in the Brexit vote. Academic researchers concluded the firm was broadly similar to its ordinary competitors and likely far less effective than the legend.

[LOWER THIRD: Sources: Fortune (2018); UK Information Commissioner's Office (2020); academic analysis (Jakee & Fink, 2024)]

**VO:** Why hammer this? Because **overstating the manipulator's power is itself a kind of manipulation** — and a trap for *us*, the people trying to stay alert. If you believe the enemy is an unbeatable mind-control machine, you give up. You stop trusting your own judgment. You assume every opinion you don't like must be the work of bots and algorithms. That helplessness and that paranoia are *exactly* the discord these operations wanted to create. The firm, the press, and even the operations themselves all benefited from the myth of their own omnipotence.

**VO:** So hold two truths at once, the grown-up way: these techniques are real, they're used against you, and you should learn them — *and* they are not magic, your mind is not putty, and naming them takes most of their power away.

## SEGMENT 5 — How it was targeted

[GFX: section header — "THE PLAYBOOK."]

**VO:** Strip away the technology and you'll recognize old friends from Block 1, plus new digital muscle. Here's the shared playbook of modern influence operations.

**VO:** **Find the fault lines.** Every society has raw nerves — race, religion, immigration, region, identity. Operators don't invent these divisions; they find the real ones and pour accelerant on them.

**VO:** **Pose as one of us.** Instead of an obvious outsider, the message comes from accounts pretending to be ordinary members of your own group — the third-party technique from Block 1, now at the scale of thousands of fake people.

**VO:** **Aim for the strongest emotion, especially anger.** Content that enrages spreads fastest, because furious people share. So the operation manufactures outrage on purpose and lets *us* do the distributing for free.

**VO:** **Target narrowly, hide widely.** Different people get different — even opposite — messages, privately, so no one sees the whole game.

[GFX: a network map — fault lines glowing, fake "neighbor" accounts seeded along them, outrage rippling outward, real users resharing.]

## SEGMENT 6 — The ten techniques (your roadmap)

[GFX: a 10-item "season map" builds, one line at a time, each with its episode number.]

**VO:** Here's where Block 2 goes. Ten techniques, one short episode each.

[ON SCREEN — numbered list, revealed line by line.]

**VO:**
11. **Microtargeting** — aiming a tailor-made message at you alone, based on your data.
12. **Bots and sockpuppets** — fake accounts faking a crowd.
13. **Manufactured outrage** — engineering anger because anger spreads.
14. **Us-versus-them and scapegoating** — turning a group into the enemy.
15. **Fear appeals and threat inflation** — making a danger feel bigger so you'll act.
16. **Name-calling and demonization** — destroying a person instead of answering them.
17. **The false dilemma** — pretending there are only two options, theirs and disaster.
18. **Whataboutism** — dodging a charge by pointing at someone else's sins.
19. **Dog whistles** — coded words that mean one thing to most, another to a target group.
20. **The firehose of falsehood** — drowning the truth in a flood of lies.

**VO:** Learn these ten and you'll have the second half of the modern persuasion toolkit — the half built for screens.

## SEGMENT 7 — Your tool, upgraded for the feed

[GFX: the "SPOT-IT" card, "digital edition."]

**VO:** Take the five questions from Episode 1 and add three for the digital age:

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST]
1. **Who really posted this — and are they who they claim?**
2. **Why is this in front of *me*, right now?** (Who chose to show it to me?)
3. **Is this designed to make me *share* while angry?**
4. *(plus the original five: who pays, what feeling, what action, what's missing, would it hold up tomorrow.)*

**VO:** Above all, remember the reality check: the goal of much of this is not to make you believe a specific lie. It's to make you angrier, lonelier, and more certain that the other half of your neighbors are monsters. The most radical thing you can do — the thing the troll farm least wants — is to stay curious, stay calm, and refuse to hate on command.
