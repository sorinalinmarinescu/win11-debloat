# Series Overview & Production Bible

This document is the "operating manual" for the whole series. Read it once before producing any episode. It defines the audience, the tone, the recurring format, the on-screen conventions, and the teaching backbone that ties all 22 episodes together.

---

## 1. The one-sentence mission

> Give an ordinary citizen the ability to **notice**, **name**, and **roughly decode** the manipulation techniques used by advertisers and politicians.

If a viewer finishes the course and, while watching a TV ad or a campaign rally, thinks *"that's a bandwagon appeal stacked on a fear appeal, and they want me angry enough to share it"* — the series has succeeded.

---

## 2. Audience assumptions

- Average high-school education. No psychology, marketing, statistics, or political-science background.
- Busy. Episodes are short, self-contained, and each ends with one practical takeaway.
- Skeptical of being lectured. We never tell people *what* to think about an issue — only *how* the persuasion is built.
- Reachable. We assume goodwill and intelligence, never that the viewer is a "sheep." Manipulation works on **everyone**, including experts and including the people who make this series. That is the humane, disarming frame.

---

## 3. Tone & voice

- **Curious, calm, and a little playful** — closer to a good science-explainer channel than to a doom-laden exposé.
- **Non-partisan and non-preachy.** We critique *methods*, not sides.
- **Honest about uncertainty.** When scholars disagree (e.g., how well micro-targeting actually works), we say so. Over-claiming would make us guilty of the very techniques we teach.
- **Empowering, not frightening.** Every episode ends by handing the viewer a tool, not a worry.

---

## 4. The recurring episode format (technique episodes)

Each of the 20 technique episodes follows the same skeleton so viewers build a habit:

1. **COLD OPEN (≈30–45s)** — A vivid hook: a mini-scenario, a real slogan, or a question. No title card yet.
2. **TITLE CARD** — Series logo + episode title + "Technique #X of 20."
3. **SEGMENT 1 — What it is.** A plain-language definition. One sentence a 15-year-old could repeat.
4. **SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain.** The psychology, explained with an everyday analogy.
5. **SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign.** A callback to Campaign A or B showing the technique in its "wild" form.
6. **SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today.** 2–3 modern examples, split between advertising and politics.
7. **SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it.** The "Spot-It Checklist": 3–4 concrete tells.
8. **SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself.** A 1-week observation challenge or a quick mental drill.
9. **RECAP + TEASE.** One-line summary, then a hook for the next episode.
10. **SOURCES.** On-screen citations.

Campaign episodes (1 and 12) use a different structure — see those scripts.

## 5. On-screen conventions (used in every script)

| Tag | Meaning |
|-----|---------|
| `**VO:**` | Voice-over narration — the words to be spoken/read by TTS. |
| `[ON SCREEN: ...]` | Full-screen text (definitions, checklists, big numbers). |
| `[GFX: ...]` | A graphic, diagram, animation, or kinetic-typography cue. |
| `[B-ROLL: ...]` | Background footage / archival imagery / stock video. |
| `[LOWER THIRD: ...]` | Caption strip: names, titles, dates, source URLs. |
| `[SFX: ...]` | Sound effect or music note. |
| `[CHECKLIST]` | The recurring "Spot-It" card; same visual style every time. |

**Citation on screen:** whenever a hard fact, date, or number appears, show a `[LOWER THIRD]` with a short source (e.g., "Source: RAND Corp., 2016"). Full URLs live in `SOURCES.md`.

## 6. The teaching backbone — three big ideas

Repeat these three ideas across the whole series so they sink in:

1. **Manipulation targets the fast brain.** Humans have a quick, automatic, emotional mode of thinking and a slow, effortful, logical mode. (Popularized as "System 1 / System 2" by psychologist Daniel Kahneman.) Almost every technique in this course works by getting you to decide with the fast brain before the slow brain wakes up.
2. **The goal is usually a feeling, not a fact.** Ask not "is this claim true?" but "what does this want me to *feel*, and what does it want me to *do* while I feel it?"
3. **The most powerful techniques hide the source.** The more an influence pretends to be your own idea, a neutral fact, or a spontaneous crowd, the more careful you should be.

---

## 7. The universal "Spot-It" questions

Every technique episode's checklist is a special case of these five questions. Teach them in Episode 1 and call back to them constantly:

1. **Who is talking, and who is paying?** (Cui bono — who benefits?)
2. **What am I being made to feel right now?** (Fear, pride, anger, belonging, desire?)
3. **What do they want me to do while I feel it?** (Buy, vote, share, hate, stay silent?)
4. **What's missing?** (The other side, the price, the evidence, the source.)
5. **Would this still convince me tomorrow, calm, in daylight?**

## 8. Suggested release plan

- Release Episode 1 (Campaign A) as the **trailer/pilot**; it should work as a standalone.
- Release the ten Block-1 technique episodes weekly.
- Release Episode 12 (Campaign B) as a **mid-season premiere**.
- Release the ten Block-2 technique episodes weekly.
- Optional **finale**: a 15-minute "Putting It All Together" episode where the viewer analyzes a fresh campaign using everything learned. (A script outline for this is included at the bottom of `EP22`.)

## 9. Standard disclaimers (show once per episode, e.g., end card)

- *"This series analyzes persuasion techniques for educational purposes. Naming a technique is not an accusation of bad faith — good causes use these tools too."*
- *"Examples are illustrative and drawn from public sources listed in the description."*

## 10. Production notes for the creator

- **Narration length:** target ~1,500–1,950 spoken words per episode ≈ 11–14 minutes at a calm ~135 words/minute, leaving room for pauses, music, and on-screen reading. Campaign episodes run slightly longer.
- **Graphics:** kinetic typography for the slogans, simple 2D motion for the "how your brain works" bits, and Ken Burns–style pans over archival images for the historical campaign.
- **Accessibility:** burn in captions; the checklists should be readable as static frames so viewers can pause and screenshot.
- **Music:** keep it neutral. Ominous music is itself an emotional-manipulation technique (covered in Episode 8 and 17) — don't use the tools we're warning against, or if you do, do it knowingly and maybe wink at it.
