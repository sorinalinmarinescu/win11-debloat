# Episode 5 — Technique #4: Pseudo-Events (Manufactured News)
### How to make news happen on purpose

**Block:** 1 · **Technique 4 of 20** · **Target runtime:** 13–14 min
**Anchor callback:** Campaign A — the Easter "Torches of Freedom" march, staged to be photographed.
**Logline:** A pseudo-event is something staged mainly so the press will report it. It looks like news happening *to* the world; really, it's news manufactured *for* you.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define the *pseudo-event* (Daniel Boorstin's term) and why it's so newsworthy.
- See why "it got covered" is not the same as "it mattered."
- Recognize the modern forms: the photo-op, the publicity stunt, the launch event, the staged outrage.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Pseudo-event:** an event that is staged primarily in order to be reported or reproduced. Its main purpose is the coverage itself.

**VO:** The term comes from the historian Daniel Boorstin, who described it in his 1962 book *The Image*. A real event happens whether or not anyone's watching — a flood, a discovery, an election result. A pseudo-event is planned *for* the watching. The press conference, the product launch, the publicity stunt, the photo-op: all built to be captured.

[LOWER THIRD: "Pseudo-event" — Daniel J. Boorstin, *The Image* (1962)]

**VO:** Pseudo-events aren't always sinister. A charity might stage one for a good cause. But they share a manipulative power: they manufacture significance. Something feels important *because* it's on the news, even though it's on the news only because someone arranged for it to be.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a loop animation — "staged" → "covered" → "feels important" → "covered more."]

**VO:** Your brain uses a rough rule: if something is everywhere — on every channel, every feed — it must matter. Coverage equals importance. Pseudo-events exploit that rule by jumping straight to the coverage, skipping the part where something genuinely important happens.

**VO:** They're also irresistible to the media, which is the clever part. A pseudo-event is tailor-made for cameras: a clear visual, a quotable line, a tidy time and place. It's *easy* to cover. So it gets covered — not because an editor judged it the most important thing in the world that day, but because it was the most convenient. The manipulator and the busy newsroom end up as accidental partners.

**VO:** And there's a hall-of-mirrors effect. Once one outlet covers it, the fact that "it's being covered" becomes its own news. The event becomes important because it's being treated as important. Boorstin's unsettling point: in a media age, we can lose track of the difference between what's real and what's merely well-staged.

## SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign

[B-ROLL: the Torches of Freedom march; flashbulbs.]

**VO:** The Easter "Torches of Freedom" march is the textbook pseudo-event — scholars literally use it that way. Nothing about it was spontaneous. Bernays chose the women, wrote the framing, tipped off the photographers, and scripted when the cigarettes would be lit. Stripped of the cameras, it was a few people smoking on a street. *With* the cameras — and the ready-made phrase "torches of freedom" — it became a national story about women and equality.

[LOWER THIRD: The 1929 march as a classic pseudo-event · Source: Journalism History (2014)]

**VO:** The newspapers reported it as something that *happened*. In truth, it was something that was *made* — made specifically so they would report it.

## SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today

[B-ROLL: montage — a product "unveiling" on a big stage; a politician touring a factory in a hard hat; a record-breaking stunt; a staged confrontation that goes viral.]

**VO:** Modern life runs on pseudo-events.

**VO:** **Advertising and business.** The midnight product launch with fans camped outside. The "world record" attempt sponsored by a brand. The "leak" that was always meant to be found. These generate free news coverage worth far more than the ad they replace — coverage that arrives wearing the costume of journalism, not advertising.

**VO:** **Politics.** The hard-hat factory tour. The signing ceremony for a bill, with the pen handed out as a souvenir. The carefully chosen backdrop — a shipyard, a border fence, a hospital. Almost none of it is the *work* of governing; it's the *imagery* of governing, produced for the evening news. Even some protests and confrontations are staged or provoked precisely to be filmed and shared.

[GFX: a "real news vs. pseudo-event" sorting game — items drop into two bins.]

**VO:** The internet supercharged this. Now anyone can manufacture an "event" — a stunt, a staged clip, a manufactured feud — designed to be shared. The currency isn't newspaper inches anymore; it's your attention and your re-share. Which means *you* are now part of the press the pseudo-event is built to capture.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Pseudo-Events]
- **Ask: "What actually happened here?"** Strip away the cameras. Is there a real event underneath, or just the staging?
- **Who arranged the coverage?** A genuine event doesn't usually come with a press kit, a hashtag, and perfect lighting.
- **Does it change anything, or just *show* something?** A ribbon-cutting shows; it doesn't build.
- **Why am I seeing this *now*?** Pseudo-events are timed — before a vote, before a launch, during a slow news cycle.

**VO:** The defense is a deflating little question you can ask of any spectacle: *"If no one had filmed this, would it have mattered at all?"* If the honest answer is no, you're looking at a pseudo-event, and you can enjoy the show without mistaking it for substance.

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, watch a news bulletin or scroll your feed and try to sort each story into two piles: "this happened on its own" versus "this was staged to be reported." Photo-ops, launches, ribbon-cuttings, stunts — into the second pile. You'll notice how much of the "news" is actually theatre. That's not cause for despair; it's just X-ray vision.
