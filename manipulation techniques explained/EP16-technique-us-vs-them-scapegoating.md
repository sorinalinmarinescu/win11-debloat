# Episode 16 — Technique #14: Us-vs-Them & Scapegoating
### How to turn neighbors into enemies

**Block:** 2 · **Technique 14 of 20** · **Target runtime:** 13–14 min
**Anchor callback:** Campaign B — pitting groups against each other and inflaming every identity.
**Logline:** Splitting the world into a virtuous "us" and a threatening "them" is one of the oldest and most dangerous moves in persuasion. Add a scapegoat — a group blamed for your problems — and you have a tool that has driven everything from sales to atrocities.

**Learning objectives:**
- Understand why the brain forms "us" and "them" almost instantly, and how that's exploited.
- Define *scapegoating* and recognize the blame-shift.
- Learn to notice when you're being recruited into a tribe against a manufactured enemy.

---

## COLD OPEN

[B-ROLL: two groups of strangers handed red or blue wristbands at random; within minutes, they cluster, eye each other warily.]

**VO:** Hand a room of strangers colored wristbands at random — red, blue — and tell them nothing else. Within minutes, people favor their own color. They trust the reds more, share with the reds more, and start to find the blues a little suspicious. No history. No reason. Just a color, assigned by a coin flip.

[Beat.]

**VO:** Psychologists have run versions of this experiment for decades. The lesson is unsettling: the line between "us" and "them" is so easy to draw that you can do it with nothing at all. Which means anyone who *wants* to divide you has very little work to do.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Us-vs-them (in-group / out-group):** framing an issue as a struggle between your good group and a threatening other group.
**Scapegoating:** blaming a person or group for problems they didn't cause, to give anger a target.

**VO:** "Us-vs-them" turns a question of *ideas* into a question of *loyalty*. Once you belong to a side, you stop weighing arguments and start defending your team. Scapegoating adds a villain: it takes complicated problems — the economy, crime, your own hard luck — and pins them on a group that's easy to resent. Complex fear becomes simple blame, and blame feels good. It's clean. It points somewhere.

**VO:** The scapegoat is usually a group that's already disliked, already a minority, or already "other" in some way — because the blame has to feel plausible to the people you're recruiting.

---

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: the red/blue wristband crowd; a "social identity" meter lighting up.]

**VO:** For almost all of human history, your group *was* your survival. The tribe fed you, defended you, and exiled you to die if you betrayed it. So evolution built us to crave belonging and to treat outsiders with caution. Researchers studying "social identity" found that we draw part of our very sense of self from the groups we belong to — which means an attack on the group feels like an attack on *you*.

[LOWER THIRD: On group identity and in-group bias — social-identity research (Tajfel and others)]

**VO:** Manipulators exploit three things this creates. **Belonging:** the warm pull to be part of the in-group. **Loyalty:** once you're in, disagreeing with the group feels like betrayal, so you self-censor. And **threat:** nothing unites a group like a common enemy. If a leader can convince you that "they" are coming for you, you'll forgive your own side almost anything and follow almost anywhere.

**VO:** Scapegoating works because blame is psychologically easier than complexity. "The economy is shaped by global forces, technology, and a hundred trade-offs" is true and unsatisfying. "*They* did this to you" is false and delicious. It hands you a villain, absolves you of any role, and gives your fear somewhere to go.

## SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign

[B-ROLL: the troll farm; opposing groups organized against each other.]

**VO:** Our Campaign B operation was, at its core, an us-vs-them machine. Its goal wasn't to make Americans love Russia; it was to make Americans loathe *each other*. It built fake communities for many different identities and then fed each one content portraying the others as dangerous, contemptuous enemies. It even arranged for opposing groups to protest the same event at the same place — manufacturing the visual of a nation at war with itself.

[LOWER THIRD: Inflaming many group identities against each other · Source: Senate Intelligence Committee reports]

**VO:** Notice it didn't pick one scapegoat. It handed *every* group a scapegoat — each other. That's the dark genius: us-vs-them doesn't need a true enemy, just a believable one, multiplied.

## SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today

[B-ROLL: montage — "real fans vs. sellouts" marketing; political ads casting the other side as an existential threat; "those people" rhetoric.]

**VO:** **Advertising.** Even brands play it: "Are you a [our brand] person or one of *them*?" Building tribal identity around a product — phone camps, car camps, console camps — turns customers into defenders who'll argue with strangers for free.

**VO:** **Politics.** This is the heavy artillery. Listen for the slide from "I disagree with their policy" to "they are not real members of this nation," "they hate you," "they want to destroy our way of life." When opponents stop being fellow citizens with bad ideas and become an existential enemy, persuasion has been replaced by mobilization. And scapegoating recurs in every era and on every side: blame the immigrants, blame the elites, blame the foreigners, blame the minority — whoever is convenient for the fear of the moment.

[GFX: a dial that slides from "I disagree with them" → "they're wrong" → "they're bad" → "they're the enemy." The last setting glows red.]

**VO:** The reliable warning sign is *dehumanizing* language — describing a group as vermin, as a disease, as a flood or an infestation. That language has preceded the worst events in human history. It is never a small thing, no matter who says it about whom.

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Us-vs-Them]
- **Loyalty over logic?** Are you being asked to *defend a side*, or to weigh an argument?
- **One simple villain for a complex problem?** Real problems have many causes. A single human scapegoat is a red flag.
- **Dehumanizing words?** Vermin, disease, flood, infestation, "not really one of us." Treat these as alarms.
- **Are "they" allowed any humanity?** Manipulators paint the out-group as uniformly evil. Reality is never that tidy.

**VO:** The defense is a deliberate act of imagination: picture a *specific, decent person* in the group being demonized — and ask whether the sweeping accusation really fits them. Stereotypes survive only at a distance. Individuals break them. Insisting on the individual is how you refuse the recruitment.

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, when you encounter "those people" rhetoric — about any group, from any side — do one thing: silently restate the claim about *one real individual* you know or can imagine from that group. "Immigrants are X." Okay — is your specific neighbor, coworker, or classmate X? The accusation usually shrinks the moment it has to fit a real face. That shrinkage is you, refusing to be divided.
