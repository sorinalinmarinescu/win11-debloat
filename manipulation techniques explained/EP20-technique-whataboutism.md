# Episode 20 — Technique #18: Whataboutism & Deflection
### Dodging the question by pointing at someone else

**Block:** 2 · **Technique 18 of 20** · **Target runtime:** 12–13 min
**Anchor callback:** Campaign B — the operation's Cold-War lineage of deflection and "what about you?"
**Logline:** Whataboutism answers a criticism not with a defense but with a counter-accusation — "what about *them*?" — so that the original charge is never actually addressed, and everyone walks away feeling everyone's equally guilty.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define *whataboutism* and the *tu quoque* fallacy.
- See how deflection protects the guilty and exhausts the honest.
- Learn to hold the original question open until it's answered.

## SEGMENT 1 — What it is

[ON SCREEN — DEFINITION]
**Whataboutism:** responding to a criticism or question with a counter-accusation ("but what about X?") instead of an answer.
**Tu quoque ("you too"):** the fallacy of dismissing a point by accusing the other side of hypocrisy.

**VO:** Whataboutism is a specific, slippery form of changing the subject. When someone is criticized, instead of defending themselves or admitting fault, they fire back: "What about the *other* side? What about that *other* time? What about *your* sins?" It feels like a counter-punch. Logically, it's a dodge — it never addresses whether the original criticism was true.

**VO:** It has a long political pedigree. It's strongly associated with Cold-War-era Soviet propaganda, where criticism from abroad would be met not with an answer but with "and what about the problems over *there*?" The tactic was so routine that it earned its mocking name. It survives because it works.

## SEGMENT 2 — Why it works on your brain

[GFX: a spotlight on "the question"; whataboutism yanks the spotlight onto a different target; the first one goes dark.]

**VO:** Whataboutism exploits a few things at once. First, **fairness instinct**: we genuinely care about consistency and hypocrisy, so "but they do it too!" *feels* like a real and relevant point. It tickles our sense of justice. Second, **tribalism**: if it's my side being criticized, I'm relieved to have a reason to fire back at yours, so I grab the deflection gratefully. Third, **attention**: there's only so much focus in a conversation, and pointing somewhere new drains it from the original question.

**VO:** The deepest damage is what whataboutism does over time. If *every* accusation is met with a counter-accusation, you slowly conclude that everyone is equally rotten, nothing can really be judged, and there's no point trying to tell better from worse. That cynical shrug — "they're all the same, it's all corrupt" — is not an accident. For some propagandists it's the entire goal: not to make you believe their side is clean, but to make you believe *no* side is, so you stop holding anyone to account.

**VO:** Notice this links back to our reality check from Campaign B. Manufactured cynicism and helplessness serve the manipulator just as well as belief does. Whataboutism is a cynicism factory.

## SEGMENT 3 — In the anchor campaign

[B-ROLL: the troll farm; content amplifying "they're all corrupt, it's all rigged."]

**VO:** Our Campaign B operation inherited this Cold-War toolkit directly. A recurring theme of modern influence operations is not "our side is virtuous" but "*everyone's* side is filthy, so why trust any of it — the elections, the media, the institutions?" Whataboutism is perfect for that. Every criticism gets met with "what about...," every standard gets muddied, until the audience throws up its hands. A population that believes nothing and trusts no one is exactly the goal of an operation built to sow discord.

## SEGMENT 4 — In the wild today

[B-ROLL: montage — a CEO deflecting to a competitor's scandal; a politician answering "what about the other party?"; an online argument spiraling into "but YOUR side..."]

**VO:** **Advertising and business.** Caught in a scandal? Point at a competitor's worse one. "Everyone in this industry does it" is whataboutism in a suit — true or not, it doesn't address whether *you* did it.

**VO:** **Politics.** This is a daily reflex across the entire spectrum. Confronted with a problem on their own side, partisans pivot instantly to the other side's equivalent or worse. Sometimes the comparison is even fair and worth making — hypocrisy is real and worth naming. But watch what it's used *for*: if "what about them?" arrives *instead of* an answer, as a way to make the original question disappear, it's a dodge, however true the counter-charge.

[GFX: a "two questions, not one" demo — the original charge stays on screen, glowing, while a second charge appears beside it; both must be answered.]

**VO:** Here's the key insight that defuses it: **two things can both be bad.** "They did something wrong too" can be completely true *and* completely irrelevant to whether *this* thing was wrong. Whataboutism tricks you into thinking you must pick one question. You don't. You can hold both.

---

## SEGMENT 5 — How to spot it

[GFX: "SPOT-IT" card.]

[ON SCREEN — CHECKLIST: Whataboutism]
- **Was the question answered?** Strip away the counter-attack. Did they actually address the original point — yes or no?
- **Answer or deflection?** "What about them?" *instead* of a response is a dodge. The same fact raised *after* answering can be fair.
- **Two things can both be bad.** A real wrong on the other side doesn't erase the wrong in front of you.
- **Cynicism check.** Is the takeaway "they're all equally guilty, so stop judging"? That conclusion is often the goal.

**VO:** The defense is gentle persistence: "That may be true, and we can talk about it — but first, what about *this*?" Keep the original spotlight on. You don't have to deny the counter-charge; you just refuse to let it bury the question that was actually asked.

---

## SEGMENT 6 — Try it yourself

[GFX: "THIS WEEK'S CHALLENGE."]

**VO:** This week, in any disagreement — online or at the dinner table — practice one sentence: "We can get to that, but let's finish this first." Notice how often a conversation tries to leap to "what about...," and how much clearer things get when you politely keep one question open until it's actually answered. It's a small act of refusing to be deflected — and it's surprisingly powerful.
